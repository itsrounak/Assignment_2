GLOBAL ECONOMY ANALYSIS

By Rounak Agarwal

Economic indicators are statistics that are used to obtain a more precise picture of the state of the economy today, as their name suggests. Few pertinent examples include the Gross Domestic Product (GDP), the Unemployment Rate, Trade, and Globalisation. When anticipating how the economy will develop over the coming years, economic data and their interpretation by reliable sources are crucial.

With the help of these forecasts, both consumers and businesses will be able to make informed choices about their long-term financial plans, including where and how much money to invest, whether to hire more people or cut back, whether to buy real estate, etc.

Famous Indicators of Economic Growth

The Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a common statistic used by countries around the world to assess how well they are doing economically. GDP is a gauge of how much money has been made through time from the production of products and services. The GDP of a nation is widely used to gauge economic health and determine whether the economy is growing or experiencing a recession.

The COVID-19 virus grew alarmingly in 2020, sickening millions of people and halting economic activity as nations implemented tight travel restrictions to stop the infection's spread. The world's largest economic shock in decades has already occurred, and the economic damage is evident as the health and human toll mounts. Virtually every nation's GDP rate has been impacted by the virus, and a 5.2 percent decline in the global GDP rate is anticipated in 2020. It is considered as the worst global recession in decades, despite unprecedented efforts by governments to counteract the downturn through fiscal and monetary policy support.

Unemployment refers to those who are employable and actively looking for work but are unable to find work. It is a key macroeconomic metric. How well our economy is going is reflected in the unemployment rate. Unemployment shows that we are not effectively using our labour, which prevents us from producing as many goods and services as we could. Additionally, unemployment has a human cost. No matter how well things are going for the average citizen, individuals who lack employment and consequently income will be in a worse situation.

Trade openness (as a share of GDP) Trade openness (as a share of GDP) refers to a country's economic orientation in the context of international trade. The actual size of an economy's registered imports and exports is used to determine its degree of openness. Trading openness is a measure of how open or closed a country's trade relations are with the rest of the world. It is viewed as a means of boosting economic development in a developing country.

Growth, job creation, and poverty reduction all depend on openness. Trade increases productivity, expands domestic business opportunities, and fosters innovation through market competition. Consequently, the GDP and other economic metrics will be impacted.

The bubble plot demonstrates that trade openness has a negative impact on growth in nations with low financial development, like Democratic Republic of Congo, while having little to no impact in countries with high financial development, like United States, Singapore, and other countries.

Other Indicators

Life satisfaction is often reported using a 'Cantril Ladder' which measures the scale of happiness from 0 to 10. Within countries, there is a strong correlation between income and happiness at specific moments in time. It has been shown that populations in wealthy nations report higher levels of average life satisfaction, and the opposite is also true.

Urbanisation and economic growth are inextricably intertwined, yet this has both beneficial and harmful effects. Economic development typically entails the conversion of agricultural property to urban purposes, such as industrial, residential, and commercial; this is a shift from an agrarian-based economy to one that is more urban in nature and centred on services and industry.

The Child Mortality Rate, also known as the under-five mortality rate, is the likelihood of dying between birth and the age of five, expressed per 1,000 live births. Recessions are thought to negatively affect mother and infant outcomes, particularly in the early stages of a nation's growth, but the effects vary widely across different systems. The creation and implementation of measures that shield disadvantaged populations from the effects of changing national income presents a challenge to today's policy makers.
